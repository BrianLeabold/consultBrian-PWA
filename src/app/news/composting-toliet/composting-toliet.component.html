Composting Toilet

March 18th, 2019

A great way to Reduce, Recycle, and Reuse.

Credit - farmingmybackyard.com/diy-composting-toilet

One of the things that attracted me to moving to California was the states initiatives in recycling, lower water usage, and helping future generations manage the the destruction my parents and I caused. So when I was approached to build a composting toilet, I was all in.

After one Google search, I found this super simple composting toilet. I will walk you through the process and the materials needed. Also, no materials were purchased for this project. I will be using repurposed materials and scraps from previous projects. I am removing a toilet from and installing this in an RV.

Material List

Your materials may differ based on your situation

  • (2) 5 gallon buckets
  • Toilet Seat & Cover
  • Saw Dust
  • (2) Formica Cabinet Doors
  • (1) 15" X 15" X 3/4" plywood
  • (4) 3/4" X 2"
  • (8) 2" X 4" X 16"
  • (16) 3" Exterior Screws
  • (32) 1 5/8" Exterior Screws
    Removing The Old Toilet

    After taking the necessary measurements, I removed the toilet. I then created a seal, using the foam gasket, a few pieces of 3/4" X 2", and a piece of 3/4" plywood. This is to ensure no gases can escape the RV's holding tank. The plywood will also serve as the base for our 5 gal. bucket will rest on. Remove the lid and seat from the toilet, you will be reusing it for the same purpose later.

    Framing Out Our Compost Toilet

    Since I am installed this in an RV, only the front and top needed to be covered. I cut my 2" X 4" to length and screwed them together, then attached them to the floor with 3" exterior grade screws. I then repurposed two formica cabinet doors. These serve as the base for the seat and cover the front, and give you an easy to clean surface.

    Final Steps

    I then measured, marked, and drilled the holes for the seat. After attaching it, I traced the inside of the seat and then added 1" to the diameter of the hole. Using a jig saw I cut out the hole and placed the 5 gallon bucket directly under the hole. Then I added 2" of sawdust to the bottom of the bucket.

    How It Works

    A composting toilet takes human waste, and dry material such as sawdust in this case, crushed leaves, or wood ash and composts it with straw at a high heat to kill potential pathogens. Instead of flushing, cover all the contents with a thick layer of cover material. The cover material is key to making the system work. When the bucket is full, put in an empty bucket and take the full bucket out to your outdoor compost bin.
